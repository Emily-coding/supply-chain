Methodology: using ONS Supply and Use Tables to assess pass-through

The ONS publishes annual Supply and Use Tables (SUT) at 105 industries × 105 products (CPA), and periodic Input-Output Analytical Tables (IOATs) at the same resolution. This is the natural instrument for turning the qualitative story in the table above into numbers.

Step 1 — direct energy cost share by industry. From the Use table, for each industry j take purchases of:

  • CPA 35.1 — Electricity, transmission and distribution
  • CPA 35.2 — Gas; distribution of gaseous fuels through mains
  • CPA 19.2 — Coke and refined petroleum products (for refining & transport)

Divide by industry j's total output. Rank percentiles to reproduce the ETII 80th-percentile threshold — but on a continuous scale, which also captures sectors just below the policy cut-off.

sj = (U35.1,j + U35.2,j + U19.2,j) / Xj

Step 2 — technical coefficients matrix A. Divide each column of the industry-by-industry Use matrix by the column's total output to give A, the intermediate input requirement per £1 of output.

A = U · diag(X)-1

Step 3 — Leontief inverse L. Total (direct + indirect) output needed across the economy to deliver £1 of final demand in industry j:

L = (I − A)-1

Step 4 — indirect energy intensity. Let e be the row vector of direct energy cost shares sj. Then the full (direct + indirect) energy content per £1 of final demand in each industry is ē = e · L. This is what actually moves when wholesale gas/electricity prices move — and it is typically 1.5–3× the direct share in downstream sectors (food processing, transport, hospitality).

Step 5 — realised pass-through. Combine ē with observed ONS Producer Price Index (PPI) and output-volume changes for 2021 Q1 → 2024 Q4:

ρj = ΔPPIj / (ēj · ΔPenergy)

ρ = 1 ⇒ full pass-through; ρ < 1 ⇒ margin compression / quantity adjustment; ρ > 1 ⇒ excess pass-through (the Lafrogne-Joussier/Martin/Méjean result for concentrated French sectors). The residual adjustment is captured in the output-volume series; by construction ΔPPI and Δvolume sum (with elasticities) to the total incidence of the shock.

Step 6 — trade-intensity overlay. Merge HMRC trade data (exports + imports) / output at SIC level. Sectors with high ē and high trade intensity are those where the SUT predicts full pass-through but competitive pressure from imports prevents it — exactly the sectors the ONS 2025 impact paper shows cutting output the most.

Practical notes: (a) the ONS Analytical IOAT is slightly lagged (latest published ~2019/2020); for the shock period use the 2021 SUT released in 2024 as the best structural base. (b) Gas used as a chemical feedstock (ammonia) sits in CPA 35.2 but is not energy in a thermodynamic sense — useful to flag separately. (c) Imported energy-intensive intermediates partially insulate downstream UK industries — capture this by splitting the Use matrix into domestic vs imported rows (ONS publishes both).
