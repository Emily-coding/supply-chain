Supply-chain energy exposures by SIC — direct + indirect

Per £1 of industry output, from the 2023 ONS A and Leontief inverse L matrices. Energy-share columns use a corrected energy-intensity vector at every domestic node — efuel[i] = Adom[fuel,i] + Aimp[fuel,i] — so direct cost shares now include imported jet kerosene, marine bunker and diesel that industries buy without going through a UK energy industry. The Leontief inverse itself remains strictly domestic, Ldom = (I − Adom)⁻¹ — i.e. supply-chain propagation passes through UK industries only. Default view shows the three fuel totals (direct + indirect via UK supply chain). Click Show direct/indirect breakdown to split each total into its direct (efuel[j], UK + imp) and UK-domestic-indirect parts. The blue-tinted column rebases direct gas+electricity to share of GVA — this is the DESNZ ETII formula (80th-percentile cutoff ≈ 8.3% in this dataset defines ETII eligibility). We don't show "Total/GVA" because Leontief total embodies energy across the whole supply chain (output-scaled numerator) over a much smaller value-added denominator — it produces >100% values that aren't economically interpretable as "energy share of value added". Imp% is the share of j's intermediate input spending that is imported — a flag for how much of the upstream supply chain is unmeasured (foreign embodied energy in imported non-energy intermediates). ρ is calibrated pass-through; the badge is the implied adjustment margin.

Literature estimates of price / quantity adjustment. Pass-through. Ganapati, Shapiro & Walker (AEJ Applied 2020), US manufacturing micro-data: ~70% of energy-cost changes pass into output prices on average, >100% in concentrated sectors, <70% in trade-exposed commodity segments. Lafrogne-Joussier, Martin & Méjean (CEPII 2023), French firm-level: ~100% pass-through of energy costs when firms reset prices, ~115% in least-competitive sectors (excess pass-through under market power). Bank of England / Bank Underground (2023): UK aggregate pass-through is gradual, incomplete, and asymmetric.

Energy demand price elasticity (quantity margin). Marin & Vona (Eur. Econ. Rev. 2021), French manufacturing firms: price elasticity ≈ −0.4 (electricity), −0.9 (gas); shrinks to −0.2 / −0.7 in the 2022 large-shock regime. Labandeira, Labeaga & López-Otero (Energy Policy 2017), meta-analysis of 428 studies: short-run ≈ −0.21, long-run ≈ −0.61. Adeyemi-Hunt-style OECD industrial electricity panels: short-run ≈ −0.11 to −0.24, long-run ≈ −0.11 to −0.28; output elasticity ≈ 0.5. Adjustment-channel ranking (Lafrogne-Joussier et al.): cut energy input → raise prices → substitute toward imported intermediates → shift production across plants → invest in efficiency.
Upstream-supply-chain undercount — where the residual MRIO gap matters. The corrected energy-intensity vector adds direct imports of energy products (jet kerosene, marine bunker, diesel, imported gas, electricity interconnector) to each industry's direct cost share, and propagates that correction through the UK domestic Leontief — so imported jet fuel at H51 flows correctly into industries that buy air transport. What it still does not capture is foreign embodied energy in imported non-energy intermediates (e.g. Chinese steel bought by UK fabricators, German chemicals bought by UK plastics, imported semiconductors bought by UK electronics manufacturers). Closing that gap requires a multi-region input-output table (OECD-ICIO, Eora) — out of scope here. The Imp% column flags the share of each industry's intermediate input spending that is imported, which is a proxy for the size of the residual undercount. Industries where Imp% > 35% are the ones whose true total energy footprint is most likely to be materially higher than the table reports — Hardt et al. (2018) put the average UK industrial MRIO uplift at 20–30% across all goods-producing sectors, with concentration in the high-import-intensity tail. Top of the watch list: Aircraft repair (C3316) ~71%, Other chemicals (C205) ~59%, Rubber & plastics (C22) ~53%, Petrochemicals (C20B) ~52%, Aluminium & other basic metals (C244_5) ~51%, Edible oils (C104) ~50%, Wood products (C16) ~48%, Industrial gases / fertilisers (C20A) ~46%, Air transport (H51) ~46%, Dyestuffs & agrochemicals (C20C) ~44%, Paints (C203) ~44%, Iron & steel (C241T243) ~42%, Motor vehicles (C29) ~42%, Electronics (C26) ~36%.

Methodology: Leontief price-side IRF from ONS Supply and Use Tables

Data source. ONS Input-Output Analytical Tables, Industry by Industry, reference year 2023 (Blue Book 2025 vintage). 101 non-energy industries × 101 industries. The A coefficient matrix (direct input requirements per £1 of output) is taken directly from ONS sheet "A"; the long-run Leontief inverse L = (I−A)⁻¹ from sheet "Leontief".

Energy partition. Three industries are treated as exogenous price-setters whose output prices are shocked: C19 (Coke & refined petroleum), D351 (Electricity generation, transmission & distribution), D352_3 (Gas manufacture & distribution). All other 101 industries are endogenous.

Long-run price model. Partition A into energy (E) and rest (R) blocks. With no change in primary-input costs elsewhere, the steady-state response of non-energy producer prices to an energy shock ΔpE is:

ΔpR = (I − ARR′)⁻¹ · AER′ · ΔpE

This is the full pass-through ceiling — every firm instantly and fully passes costs on.

Dynamic IRF (Neumann series). Real-world price adjustment is staggered. The round-by-round expansion, where each round ≈ one quarter, is:

ΔpR(1) = AER′ ΔpE  (direct impact)
ΔpR(k) = AER′ ΔpE + ARR′ ΔpR(k−1)  (adds one supply-chain step per quarter)
limk→∞ ΔpR(k) = (I − ARR′)⁻¹ AER′ ΔpE

Pass-through regimes. "Full" applies ρ = 1 everywhere. "Realistic" applies sector-specific ρⱼ ∈ [0.35, 1.0] as a round-level dampener, calibrated to trade exposure and market power. Commodity, trade-exposed EII sectors (steel 0.35, non-ferrous metals 0.35, industrial gases 0.40, glass/ceramics 0.50, paper 0.55) reflect the Ganapati et al. incomplete pass-through result (~70% avg US manufacturing, lower for competitive industries). Food, utilities and services (ρ = 0.90–1.00) reflect LMM's near-complete pass-through for domestically-oriented concentrated sectors. The gap between full and realistic at q12 is the portion absorbed via margin compression or output reduction.

Shock scenarios. Unit: +10% to each energy product price simultaneously. 2022-calibrated: refined petroleum +50%, electricity +200%, gas +250% — broadly matching peak wholesale moves from 2021 Q1 to 2022 Q3. Results scale linearly with shock size.

Adjustment margin classification. Derived from ρⱼ: ρ ≤ 0.55 → Quantity-led (large residual absorbed in output/margins); 0.55 < ρ < 0.85 → Mixed; ρ ≥ 0.85 → Price-led. Corroborated by ONS (2025): energy-intensive manufacturing output −33.6% from 2021 Q1 to 2024 Q4.

Source: ONS Input-Output Analytical Tables, Industry × Industry, 2023 reference year (Blue Book 2025). Pass-through calibration: Ganapati/Shapiro/Walker (AEJ Applied 2020); Lafrogne-Joussier/Martin/Méjean (CEPII 2023). See uk-energy-exposure.md for full reference list.
