What the literature says about how firms respond to energy shocks

Price pass-through. US manufacturing micro-data (Ganapati, Shapiro & Walker, AEJ Applied 2020): firms pass through about 70% of energy-cost rises on average — more than 100% in concentrated industries, less than 70% in commodity / trade-exposed segments. French firm-level data (Lafrogne-Joussier, Martin & Méjean, CEPII 2023): closer to 100% on energy costs when firms reset prices, up to 115% in the least-competitive sectors (excess pass-through under market power). Bank of England (2023) finds UK aggregate pass-through is gradual, incomplete and asymmetric.

Quantity margin — how firms cut their own energy use. French manufacturing (Marin & Vona, Eur. Econ. Rev. 2021): price elasticity of demand ≈ −0.4 for electricity and −0.9 for gas; smaller in absolute value (−0.2 and −0.7) under the very large 2022 shock. Meta-analysis of 428 studies (Labandeira, Labeaga & López-Otero, Energy Policy 2017): short-run ≈ −0.21, long-run ≈ −0.61. OECD industrial-electricity panels (Adeyemi/Hunt-style): short-run −0.11 to −0.24, long-run −0.11 to −0.28; output elasticity ≈ 0.5. Lafrogne-Joussier et al. rank the channels firms actually use: cut energy input → raise prices → switch to imported inputs → shift production across plants → invest in efficiency.

About this table — source, column guide and what's not captured

Source. Loading ONS IxI data…

Each row is one of the IOAT industries. All percentages are share of that industry's gross output at basic prices, except the blue-tinted column which is share of GVA. Gas / Elec / Oil show the total embedded energy share — what the industry buys directly (from UK energy industries plus imported jet kerosene, marine bunker, diesel, gas and electricity) plus what's embedded in everything else it buys through the UK domestic supply chain. Click Show direct/indirect breakdown to split each total into its direct and indirect parts. Gas+Elec / GVA rebases direct gas and electricity to value added — this matches the DESNZ ETII formula; in this dataset the 80th-percentile threshold is about 8.3%. Imp% is the share of an industry's intermediate spending that is imported — used as a flag for the part of the upstream supply chain we can't track (see below). The Adjustment margin badge says whether an industry is more likely to raise prices or cut output when energy gets more expensive; hover for the ρ value behind it.

What this table doesn't capture — the upstream supply-chain gap

Imports of energy products (jet kerosene, marine bunker, diesel, imported gas, electricity interconnector) are added to each industry's direct cost share and propagate through the UK domestic supply chain — so imported jet fuel bought by UK airlines correctly flows on to industries that buy air-transport services. What is not captured is the energy embedded in imported non-energy goods — Chinese steel, German chemicals, Asian electronics. Tracking that would need a multi-region input-output table (OECD-ICIO, Eora) and is out of scope here.

The Imp% column is a flag for how big this gap is likely to be for any given industry: it is the share of the industry's intermediate spending that is imported. Sort the table by Imp% descending to find the most affected industries — aircraft repair sits at the top around 70%, then a block of chemicals, plastics, basic metals, motor vehicles and machinery in the 40–55% range; electronics is just behind at the mid-30s. Hardt et al. (2018) estimate the average UK industrial uplift from a full MRIO treatment at 20–30%, concentrated in this high-import-intensity tail.
