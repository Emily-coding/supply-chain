Supply-chain energy exposures by SIC — direct + indirect

Per £1 of industry output, from the 2023 ONS A and Leontief inverse L matrices. Energy-share columns use a corrected energy-intensity vector at every domestic node — efuel[i] = Adom[fuel,i] + Aimp[fuel,i] — so direct cost shares now include imported jet kerosene, marine bunker and diesel that industries buy without going through a UK energy industry. The Leontief inverse itself remains strictly domestic, Ldom = (I − Adom)⁻¹ — i.e. supply-chain propagation passes through UK industries only. Default view shows the three fuel totals (direct + indirect via UK supply chain). Click Show direct/indirect breakdown to split each total into its direct (efuel[j], UK + imp) and UK-domestic-indirect parts. The blue-tinted column rebases direct gas+electricity to share of GVA — this is the DESNZ ETII formula (80th-percentile cutoff ≈ 8.3% in this dataset defines ETII eligibility). We don't show "Total/GVA" because Leontief total embodies energy across the whole supply chain (output-scaled numerator) over a much smaller value-added denominator — it produces >100% values that aren't economically interpretable as "energy share of value added". Imp% is the share of j's intermediate input spending that is imported — a flag for how much of the upstream supply chain is unmeasured (foreign embodied energy in imported non-energy intermediates). The badge is the implied adjustment margin under an energy-cost shock; hover for ρ value.

Literature estimates of price / quantity adjustment. Pass-through. Ganapati, Shapiro & Walker (AEJ Applied 2020), US manufacturing micro-data: ~70% of energy-cost changes pass into output prices on average, >100% in concentrated sectors, <70% in trade-exposed commodity segments. Lafrogne-Joussier, Martin & Méjean (CEPII 2023), French firm-level: ~100% pass-through of energy costs when firms reset prices, ~115% in least-competitive sectors (excess pass-through under market power). Bank of England / Bank Underground (2023): UK aggregate pass-through is gradual, incomplete, and asymmetric.

Energy demand price elasticity (quantity margin). Marin & Vona (Eur. Econ. Rev. 2021), French manufacturing firms: price elasticity ≈ −0.4 (electricity), −0.9 (gas); shrinks to −0.2 / −0.7 in the 2022 large-shock regime. Labandeira, Labeaga & López-Otero (Energy Policy 2017), meta-analysis of 428 studies: short-run ≈ −0.21, long-run ≈ −0.61. Adeyemi-Hunt-style OECD industrial electricity panels: short-run ≈ −0.11 to −0.24, long-run ≈ −0.11 to −0.28; output elasticity ≈ 0.5. Adjustment-channel ranking (Lafrogne-Joussier et al.): cut energy input → raise prices → substitute toward imported intermediates → shift production across plants → invest in efficiency.
Upstream-supply-chain undercount — where the residual MRIO gap matters. The corrected energy-intensity vector adds direct imports of energy products (jet kerosene, marine bunker, diesel, imported gas, electricity interconnector) to each industry's direct cost share, and propagates that correction through the UK domestic Leontief — so imported jet fuel at H51 flows correctly into industries that buy air transport. What it still does not capture is foreign embodied energy in imported non-energy intermediates (e.g. Chinese steel bought by UK fabricators, German chemicals bought by UK plastics, imported semiconductors bought by UK electronics manufacturers). Closing that gap requires a multi-region input-output table (OECD-ICIO, Eora) — out of scope here. The Imp% column flags the share of each industry's intermediate input spending that is imported, which is a proxy for the size of the residual undercount. Industries where Imp% > 35% are the ones whose true total energy footprint is most likely to be materially higher than the table reports — Hardt et al. (2018) put the average UK industrial MRIO uplift at 20–30% across all goods-producing sectors, with concentration in the high-import-intensity tail.
