What the shock is, and what the numbers mean

Three steps: (1) define the energy shock, (2) propagate it through the 2023 IxI input–output matrix to get a predicted per-industry producer-price move, (3) bridge the per-industry PPI moves to CPI using household-expenditure shares. We compare each step to what actually happened in 2021–24.

Step 1 — the shock

Let sk be the percent change in the wholesale price of energy product k between 2021-01 and its 2022 peak (or average). We use two variants:

  • Internal shock (default): sk = ONS PPI Output Domestic for k ∈ {C19 refined petroleum, D351 electricity, D352_3 gas}. Peak moves ≈ +176 / +111 / +186%. This is downstream of wholesale and includes any retail/regulatory smoothing.
  • External shock (gas only, experimental): sD352_3 = Ofgem monthly NBP day-ahead gas price. Peak +503% (Aug 2022). Electricity and oil kept internal for now.

Step 2 — Leontief propagation

The 2023 ONS industry-by-industry matrix A gives every industry's input cost structure. Partition it so energy industries are exogenous:

ΔpR  =  (I − ARRᵀ)−1 · AERᵀ · ΔpE

where pR are non-energy industry prices and pE are the energy shocks. The closed-form solution is the long-run full-pass-through upper bound: every industry prices every £1 of extra energy cost straight into its output. In the code this is emitted as theor_full_22 (internal shock) and theor_full_ext (external-gas shock).

Empirical pass-through is then:

ρpeak,j  =  Δ_observed_peakj  /  theor_fullj

ρ < 1 ⇒ firm absorbed some of the shock in margin or lost volume; ρ ≈ 1 ⇒ full pass-through; ρ > 1 ⇒ either "excess pass-through" under market power (Lafrogne-Joussier–Martin–Méjean 2023) or other cost shocks (wages, sterling, non-energy inputs) also lifted the price. The columns ρ_peak and ρ_peak (ext) in the industry table give the two variants.

Step 3 — CPI bridge

For each CPI division d, we weight industry PPI moves by how much of each industry's household expenditure lands in division d. The weights come from the ONS CPA↔COICOP converter (using 2019 proportions, pre-Covid) multiplied by each IOAT-CPA's 2023 household final-consumption £ from the Use table (P3 S14 column):

Δ_predicted(d)  =  Σj  (  hh_£j · propj,d  /  Σj hh_£j · propj,d  )  ·  Δ_PPIj

ρj is not applied again here — it was already used inside Δ_PPIj. The "gap" between observed and predicted for a division captures (a) industries we haven't wired in (services gaps), (b) retail/transport margins, and (c) the crudeness of using 2019 expenditure weights against 2022 shock proportions.

Reading the tables

Energy cards

  • Peak: max monthly index value between 2021-01 and 2024-12, expressed as % change vs 2021-01.
  • Avg 2022: mean of the 12 monthly values in 2022, % vs 2021-01. This is the shock actually fed into the Leontief propagation.
  • Weighted headline PPI: Σⱼ wj × PPIj / Σⱼ wj across all 78 matched industries, where wj = industry j's 2023 gross output at basic prices (IOT!P1). Replaces the simple mean.

Bridge table

  • Observed: % change in ONS CPI division index from 2021-01 to its peak in 2021–24.
  • Predicted: Σj (sharej,d) × Δ_PPI_peakj using 2019 household-expenditure weights.
  • Gap: predicted − observed, in percentage points. Positive = bridge over-predicts (usually because a large chunk of the division's CPI weight sits in an industry the bridge hasn't seen: imputed rent, public-sector services, retail margins).
  • N: number of IOAT industries contributing to the division.

Industry table

  • Direct energy share: agas + aelec + aoil from column j of the 2023 A matrix (£ of energy per £ of output).
  • HH share of output: how much of this industry's gross output is sold directly to UK households (Use!P3 S14 ÷ IOT!P1).
  • Obs peak PPI: peak PPI (or SPPI, for services) move 2021-01 → any month 2021–24, rebased.
  • ρpeak: Δ_observed_peak ÷ theoretical-full-pass-through prediction from the avg-2022 internal shock. Below 1 ⇒ partial pass-through; above 1 ⇒ other cost pressure or excess pass-through.
  • ρpeak (ext): same but using Ofgem wholesale gas in place of D352_3 PPI. Systematically smaller because the wholesale denominator is larger (+258% avg-2022 vs +116%).
  • Agg method: ONS agg = ONS's own bucket-level aggregate series; mean of components = simple mean across 4-digit CPA PPIs feeding the bucket (no 4-digit weight data exists); single = only one component series; sppi_exact = matched SPPI at the bucket's own CPA.

Caveats

  • SPPI coverage is incomplete. 27 services industries are in via ONS SPPI (quarterly, held flat across months). Still missing: owner-occupiers' housing (imputed rent — dominates CPI 04 housing weight), health, education retail, R&D, travel agencies, cultural/recreational services, public admin, construction output. CPI division 04 is still over-predicted for this reason — the sub-divisions 04.3/04.4/04.5 themselves are fine.
  • Retail / transport margins ignored. The bridge treats factory-gate PPI as the consumer-facing price. Any margin change between 2021 and 2024 shows up as "gap" rather than being modelled.
  • Within-bucket 4-digit CPA weights are not available. Where an IOAT bucket contains multiple 4-digit CPA PPI series and ONS does not publish a bucket-level aggregate, we use simple mean across components. 28/51 buckets use the ONS aggregate directly; 20 fall back to simple mean.
  • N=1 shock. The 2022 episode is a single correlated gas/oil/electricity event; ρ estimates are conditional on that shock, not a structural parameter. Co-moving sterling depreciation and post-Covid demand are confounders.
  • Internal shock definition. The Leontief "shock" is currently the UK PPI move for D351/D352_3/C19, which already bakes in retail/regulatory pass-through. Switching to external wholesale (DESNZ NBP gas, N2EX baseload electricity, BoE Brent sterling) would separate upstream from downstream pass-through.
  • CPA→COICOP proportions from 2019. Latest pre-Covid year; basket shifts since then (substitution away from electricity, lockdown-era digital spending) are not captured in the weights. We use 2019 instead of 2020 specifically to avoid the Covid distortion.
  • Leontief is a long-run upper bound. theor_full assumes every industry prices every £1 of extra energy cost straight into its output price with no lag — no margin compression, no volume loss, no substitution. Reality is closer to 50–70% for trade-exposed commodity manufacturing (Ganapati, Shapiro & Walker 2020) and ~100–115% for concentrated / domestic-oriented sectors (LMM 2023). This is why ρ is almost always < 1 for commodity industries and > 1 for food / pharma / industrial gases.
  • Rescaling theor_full by the mean shock is a linear approximation. The code takes the IRF scenario's long-run response to +10% on all three energy products and rescales by the simple mean of the 2022 shocks. A proper calculation would propagate each energy shock separately through the Leontief inverse; with large heterogeneous shocks (gas +258% external vs electricity +51% internal) this approximation overstates ρ for gas-heavy industries and understates it for electricity-heavy ones.
  • One-shock identification. The 2022 episode is a single correlated gas/oil/electricity event with co-moving sterling depreciation and post-Covid demand. ρ is conditional on that shock, not a structural parameter. External Ofgem gas helps separate upstream from downstream, but doesn't solve the N=1 problem — the wholesale moves of gas, TTF, oil and electricity were themselves nearly collinear.
  • Gas is a feedstock, not just fuel, for chemicals / fertilisers. The A matrix captures this via £-flows but assumes fixed proportions (no substitution away from gas in ammonia, urea, methanol production).
  • External shock only covers gas. Electricity and oil in the "ext" variant still use UK D351/C19 PPI as the shock source. The full external-shock story needs N2EX wholesale electricity + Brent-in-sterling to be wired in alongside Ofgem.
