Methodology: Leontief price-side IRF from ONS Supply and Use Tables

Data source. ONS Input-Output Analytical Tables, Industry by Industry, reference year 2023 (Blue Book 2025 vintage). 101 non-energy industries × 101 industries. The A coefficient matrix (direct input requirements per £1 of output) is taken directly from ONS sheet "A"; the long-run Leontief inverse L = (I−A)⁻¹ from sheet "Leontief".

Energy partition. Three industries are treated as exogenous price-setters whose output prices are shocked: C19 (Coke & refined petroleum), D351 (Electricity generation, transmission & distribution), D352_3 (Gas manufacture & distribution). All other 101 industries are endogenous.

Imports correction in AER. The energy → non-energy block uses an imports-corrected energy-intensity vector at each domestic node: efuel[j] = Adom[fuel,j] + Aimp[fuel,j], so an industry's price response to a shock reflects all the energy it buys (UK + imports), not just what it buys from UK energy industries. Imported jet kerosene at H51 propagates correctly to industries that buy air transport. The non-energy → non-energy block ARR stays domestic-A only — we don't have foreign A coefficients for non-energy intermediates. Foreign embodied energy in imported steel / chemicals / electronics is not captured.

Long-run price model. Partition A into energy (E) and rest (R) blocks. With no change in primary-input costs elsewhere, the steady-state response of non-energy producer prices to an energy shock ΔpE is:

ΔpR = (I − ARR′)⁻¹ · AER′ · ΔpE

This is the full pass-through ceiling — every firm instantly and fully passes costs on.

Dynamic IRF (Neumann series). Real-world price adjustment is staggered. The round-by-round expansion, where each round ≈ one quarter, is:

ΔpR(1) = AER′ ΔpE  (direct impact)
ΔpR(k) = AER′ ΔpE + ARR′ ΔpR(k−1)  (adds one supply-chain step per quarter)
limk→∞ ΔpR(k) = (I − ARR′)⁻¹ AER′ ΔpE

Pass-through regimes. "Full" applies ρ = 1 everywhere. "Realistic" applies sector-specific ρⱼ ∈ [0.35, 1.0] as a round-level dampener, calibrated to trade exposure and market power. Commodity, trade-exposed EII sectors (steel 0.35, non-ferrous metals 0.35, industrial gases 0.40, glass/ceramics 0.50, paper 0.55) reflect the Ganapati et al. incomplete pass-through result (~70% avg US manufacturing, lower for competitive industries). Food, utilities and services (ρ = 0.90–1.00) reflect LMM's near-complete pass-through for domestically-oriented concentrated sectors. The gap between full and realistic at q12 is the portion absorbed via margin compression or output reduction.

Shock scenarios. Unit: +10% on each energy product simultaneously — use this for elasticity-style reasoning (outputs scale linearly, so any other small shock is just a rescale). Oil-led stress: refined petroleum +100%, electricity +50%, gas +50%. A forward-looking disturbance, not a realised move — sized like a Strait of Hormuz / OPEC+ severe-cut episode (oil doubles from ~$80/bbl baseline; utilities co-move via European LNG and the marginal-generator pricing). Comparable in magnitude to 2022 but with the lead-energy reversed: 2022 was gas-led (gas +250% peak, elec +200%, oil +50%); this scenario is oil-led with milder utilities. Values are tunable in build_irf.py.

Adjustment margin classification. Derived from ρⱼ: ρ ≤ 0.55 → Quantity-led (large residual absorbed in output/margins); 0.55 < ρ < 0.85 → Mixed; ρ ≥ 0.85 → Price-led. Corroborated by ONS (2025): energy-intensive manufacturing output −33.6% from 2021 Q1 to 2024 Q4.

Why the IRF doesn't equal "Total energy × shock" from the exposures table. The exposures table's Total gas / elec / oil columns measure physical embedded energy — how much of each fuel is consumed across the whole supply chain per £1 of an industry's output, traced through every chain, including chains that pass through other energy industries. It uses the full Leontief inverse Lfull = (I − A)⁻¹ over all 104 industries (energy and non-energy together).

The IRF, by contrast, treats energy prices as exogenous: when you set "electricity +50%", that 50% is the entire output-price move of UK electricity, already reflecting any oil-cost or gas-cost pressures hitting electricity generators. The IRF formula uses only the non-energy block: ΔpR = (I − ARR′)⁻¹ · AER′ · ΔpE. Energy-to-non-energy transmission is captured by AER directly; chains that loop through other energy industries are not propagated through LRR because we've taken those energy prices as given.

If you instead compute (Total oil × oil-shock) + (Total elec × elec-shock) + (Total gas × gas-shock), every chain that physically passes through an energy industry is counted twice — once at the oil shock (via the embedded path through electricity) and again at the elec shock (because the elec shock already captures oil-cost pass-through into electricity). For Industrial gases / fertilisers (C20A) the long-run IRF response is about 8% but a naive Total × shock multiplication gives ~18%, an inflation of more than 100% driven entirely by this double-counted oil-via-electricity-via-gas chain. The IRF long-run is the right figure for a price-side IRF; the Total energy column is the right figure for carbon-accounting / LCA, where you want to attribute every joule of embedded energy to a single source independent of any specific price scenario.
